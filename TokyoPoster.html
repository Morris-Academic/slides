How Conspiracy Theories and Misinformation Overlapped in the Debate of Covid-19 Alternative Drug: A Case Study of “Ivermectin” on Facebook

Morris Sheng-hui Yang1


1 College of Communication, National Chengchi University, Taiwan.

1. Intro

The case study selected for this research is the 2021 discussion of Ivermectin on Facebook. This choice was made due to the unique socio-temporal context where official narratives on social media were in competition with alternative medicines.

The dataset contains instances of misinformation that threatened public health and government policies. For example, some claimed that Ivermectin could substitute for vaccines or lockdown and screening measures. In some cases, this misinformation evolved into conspiracy theories, alleging that the government and commercial organizations are secretly conspiring and profiting from suppressing Ivermectin. This dataset also highlights the coexistence of misinformation and conspiracy theories, a rare comparison in prior research.

2. Method

Data

For data collection and processing, CrowdTangle API (Meta) was used to crawl roughly 400,000 Facebook posts from Jan 1st, 2021, to Dec 31st, 2021, that contained the English word “Ivermectin”, including posts in different languages. After filtering out non-English posts, I was left with a dataset of 40,621 full English posts.

LDA Tuning (K-search)

To fit the LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation) model, we need to determine the number of topics, denoted as K. Various tests. In this study, I employ three commonly used methods to evaluate the performance of the topic model at different values of K (Cao et al., 2009; Arun et al., 2010; Griffiths & Steyvers, 2004).

The three curves in the graph represent the results of fitting the LDA model for each K value, ranging from 20 to 120 with an interval of 5 (Figure 1). The point of intersection of the upper and lower bounds in the graph indicates a suitable choice. In cases where there are multiple optimal solutions, the inflection point of the curve serves as the appropriate selection. In this study, I select K=75 as the optimal number of topics.

3. Result

LDA Topic Modeling

After determining K, I used LDA to categorize 38,394 posts into 75 topics (5% remained unclassified). The initial 80% of posts (N = 30,927) related to 46 topics. Coders labeled and categorized these 46 topics into three groups, excluding two due to irrelevance (N=44).

Classification coding process

Five individuals coded the content, guided by 2021 Ivermectin-related events, with extensive discussions to reach a consensus. All agreed on the final classifications. Using LDA topic modeling, we conducted a two-part coding process.

In the first part, we selected the top ten groups out of 44 with the highest post counts and encoded the top twenty posts with the highest gamma values for Figure 2.

In the second part, we used the top twenty posts with the highest gamma values to categorize the 44 topic groups into three categories: misinformation (misinfo), factual content (fact), and conspiracy theories (CT) regarding Ivermectin. This resulted in distinct colors across Figures 2 to 4.

4. Discussion and Conclusion

Top 10 Topics’ Trends Between Three Groups

The big pharma conspiracy theory had a steady and significant impact in 2021 (Figure 2). Conspiracy theory discourse on the Joe Rogan podcast emerged after the pandemic worsened and continued to gain momentum after its release. The widespread dissemination of these theories can be attributed to their long histories, the fear climate during the pandemic, and the influence of celebrities and viral dissemination.

Linguistic Psychoanalysis of Three Groups

In the LIWC dictionary analysis (Figure 4), I focused on seven vocabulary sets previously studied in conspiracy research (Fong et al., 2021). Combining LIWC word occurrences with an ANOVA test, I found that the misinfo group and fact group displayed similar levels of anger, indicative of political polarization.

Except for “anger,” all other word categories showed significant differences in average frequency of usage between the fact group and the other two groups (p-value=0), except for Pronouns, Impersonal Pronouns, and We, which had p-values greater than 0. These results suggest that the conspiracy group and misinformation group are closer in certain specific vocabulary usage.
