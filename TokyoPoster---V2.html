How Conspiracy Theories and Misinformation Overlapped in the Debate of Covid-19 Alternative Drug: A Case Study of “Ivermectin” on Facebook

Morris Sheng-hui Yang1


1 College of Communication, National Chengchi University, Taiwan.

1. Intro

This case study focuses on the 2021 Ivermectin discussion on Facebook, chosen for its unique context of official narratives clashing with alternative medicines on social media. This study presents a valuable opportunity to compare the subtle differences between conspiracy theories and misinformation within discussions about Ivermectin on various aspects.

RQ1: What are the top topics, the stances (support or opposition) in discussions about ivermectin? Any connections to conspiracy theories?

RQ2: How do the topics related to ivermectin change over time for fact, misinformation, and CT groups, under different news events?

RQ3: Do groups with different opinions use distinct vocabulary types?

2. Method

Figure 1: Methodology Design Flowchart

Data

To gather data, I utilized the CrowdTangle API (Meta) to scrape approximately 400,000 Facebook posts from January 1, 2021, to December 31, 2021, containing the word “Ivermectin.” After filtering out non-English posts, I retained a dataset of 40,621 posts in English.

LDA Tuning (K-search)

To fit the LDA model, we have to determine the number of topics (K) for the LDA model. I employed three common evaluation methods (Cao et al., 2009; Arun et al., 2010; Griffiths & Steyvers, 2004). I selected K = 75 as the optimal number of topics (Figure 2). After determining K, I used LDA Topic Modeling method to categorize 38,394 posts into 75 topics (5% remained unclassified).

Figure 2: LDA Tuning test


Classification coding process

From the initial 80% of posts (N = 30,927), 46 topics were categorized into three groups, with two irrelevant topics excluded (N = 44). Five individuals coded the content, guided by 2021 Ivermectin-related events, and unanimously agreed on the final classifications.

The LDA model generates a beta value for each token (word) and a gamma value for each document (post) in the corpus (dataset). Beta values assist in identifying the top ten terms that most accurately represent each topic, while gamma values indicate the representativeness of a post within a topic.

This research involved a two-part coding process.

  1. We categorized the top ten topics using gamma and beta values, assigning a concise title to each topic to summarize its content (Figure 3).

  2. We used the gamma values to categorize all relevent topics (n=44) into three categories: misinformation (misinfo), factual content (fact), and conspiracy theories (CT). This resulted in distinct colors across Figure 3 to Figure 5.

3. Results and Discussion

Top 10 Topics’ Trends Between Three Groups

The big pharma conspiracy theory had a steady and significant impact in 2021 (Figure 3). Conspiracy theory discourse on the Joe Rogan podcast emerged after the pandemic worsened and continued to gain momentum after its release. The widespread dissemination of these theories can be attributed to their long histories, the fear climate during the pandemic, and the influence of celebrities and viral dissemination.

Topic Network of Three Groups

Figure 4 shows a topic model network of all coded topics marked by different groups. The dashed dividing line help us see that most of the green dots (misinfo group) were closer to the CT group, indicating that the content of the misinfo group in the entire topic network was closer in distance with the CT group.
